I came back to Japan from America when I was nine. At first, I could not keep up with the Japanese education system simply because I could not understand Kanji. I began to despise Kanji and myself because I thought I was stupid. No matter how much time and effort I put into memorizing them, they seemed to me like meaningless scribbles on paper. My returnee friends suffered the same problem. While most of them were able to adapt and learn, some became discouraged to the point of leaving Japan. However, during my college entrance exams, when I began to study Japanese significantly more, I realised that Kanji is not meaningless; it is full of meaning. The radicals that make up Kanji tell a story, and memorising Kanji is memorising that story and piecing it together. I began to think that Kanji was art, beautiful and meaningful. As an educator myself, I thought the best way to teach people like me was not the Japanese way of repetition, but to show how the radicals tell a story and help them “discover” kanji by piecing it together themselves through puzzles, making learning Kanji enjoyable and meaningful.

アクセシビリティはデザインプロセスにどのように反映されましたか？
How did accessibility factor into your design process?
Accessibility was one of the most important considerations in developing this app. The challenges of learning kanji are shared by everyone, regardless of ability or disability. Therefore, this app contains extensive features designed to reduce the inclusivity gap for visually impaired and cognitively impaired users. The app’s interface and features can be customized to meet their individual needs. For visually impaired users, the puzzles and overall UI are designed with clear spacing, strong contrast, and thoughtful use of color to make each component easier to distinguish. In addition, the app makes extensive use of the Accessibility API and is designed to work seamlessly with VoiceOver to provide a smooth experience. For cognitively impaired users, the app streamlines interactions, making it easier to access and enjoy its core features. Most importantly, this app strives to make Japanese—one of the most complex languages in the world—more accessible by teaching kanji through an intuitive and enjoyable method that everyone can understand. In a society that has not yet fully recognized the importance of inclusivity, I believe it is essential to create tools that make learning accessible to everyone, and I believe this app reflects that value.

Yesなら：使用したAIツール、その用途、そしてそこから学んだことを説明してください。
Describe which AI tools you used, what you used them for, and what you learned.


I developed this app using Codex and ChatGPT. This was my first time using Codex or any agent-based CLI, and I was amazed by how much faster app development has become since I first started programming five years ago. For example, finding reference code for the new FoundationModel API became much easier because Codex could generate examples for me. AI also became an effective teacher, helping me understand concepts such as guided generation and making it easier to debug projects with many interconnected parts.
I learned how to write clearer prompts and how to use AI beyond programming to improve my productivity. I also became aware of the risks of relying on it too heavily, since overusing AI can reduce the valuable learning that comes from solving errors on my own. Therefore, I used Codex mainly for routine tasks, like adding new kanji, while I focused on designing the app’s core features. I also used AI as a practical testing tool. While I could gather feedback on the visual impairment mode from a nearly legally blind friend, I could not find anyone to test the cognitive impairment mode, so AI helped me simulate feedback, evaluate usability, and refine the experience.

アプリプレイグラウンドで他にどのような技術を使用し、なぜそれらを選択しましたか？
What other technologies did you use in your app playground, and why did you choose them?


I chose to use the Accessibility API to help reduce the accessibility gap for visually impaired users and ensure that VoiceOver works seamlessly throughout the app. I also used the FoundationModel API to let users ask a chatbot assistant for more specific hints about the target kanji. Through my experience teaching middle and high school students, I have found that the cycle of asking a question, receiving an answer, forming a hypothesis, and testing it leads to much stronger knowledge retention. By allowing users to ask more specific questions through the chatbot, this app promotes active learning rather than passive memorization, helping users build a deeper and more lasting understanding of kanji.
In addition, I used Core Graphics to generate the puzzle grid, which allowed for more realistic interactions and more precise snap detection for puzzle pieces, improving the overall user experience. Finally, I used the system font to render the kanji characters. I had originally planned to use online images of the kanji, but many of them could not be used because of copyright restrictions. Using system fonts provided a clean, reliable, and legally safe alternative.
